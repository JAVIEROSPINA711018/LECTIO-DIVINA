Exegesis on Genesis 1:1-3

Introduction

Genesis 1:1-3 is foundational for understanding the biblical account of creation. These verses

introduce the creation narrative, emphasizing God's sovereignty and creative power. The

passage has been central to theological discussions about the nature of God, the origin of the

universe, and the relationship between God and creation. This exegesis will provide a detailed

analysis of the text through literal translation, textual criticism, source evaluation, form, and

interpretation.

Literal Translation:

Verse 1:

( ְּב ֵר אִׁש יתberēʾšît) – "In the beginning" (prepositional prefix " ְּבin" + noun ֵר אִׁש ית

"beginning")

( ָּב ָר אbārāʾ) – "He created" (3rd person masculine singular perfect verb)

( ֱא ֹלִה יםʾĕlōhîm) – "God" (plural noun with singular agreement, indicating majesty or

sovereignty)

( ֵא תʾēt) – Untranslatable particle marking the direct object

( ַה ָּׁש ַמ ִיםhaššāmayim) – "The heavens" (plural definite article prefix " ַהthe" + noun)

( ְו ֵא תwĕʾēt) – "And" + direct object marker

( ָה ָאֶר ץhāʾāreṣ) – "The earth" (definite article prefix " ָהthe" + noun " ֶא ֶר ץearth")

Verse 2:

( ְו ָה ָאֶר ץwĕhāʾāreṣ) – "And the earth"

( ָה ְי ָת הhāyĕtāh) – "Was" (3rd person feminine singular perfect)

( ֹתהּוtōhû) – "Formless" or "chaotic"

( ָו ֹבהּוwābōhû) – "And void" or "empty"

( ְו ֹחֶׁש ְךwĕḥōšeḵ) – "And darkness"

( ַע ל־ְּפ ֵנ יʿal-pĕnê) – "Upon the surface of"

( ְת הֹוםtĕhôm) – "The deep" or "primeval ocean"

( ְו רּוַחwĕrûaḥ) – "And the Spirit" or "wind"

( ֱא ֹלִה יםʾĕlōhîm) – "God"

( ְמ ַר ֶח ֶפ תmĕraḥep̱ eṯ) – "Was hovering" (participle feminine singular)

( ַע ל־ְּפ ֵנ יʿal-pĕnê) – "Over the surface of"

( ַה ָּמ ִיםhammāyim) – "The waters"

Verse 3:

( ַו ֹּיאֶמ רwayyōʾmer) – "And said" (3rd person masculine singular imperfect)

( ֱא ֹלִה יםʾĕlōhîm) – "God"

( ְי ִה יyĕhî) – "Let there be" (jussive verb)

( אֹורʾôr) – "Light"

( ַו ְי ִה יwayĕhî) – "And there was"

( אֹורʾôr) – "Light"

Textual Criticism:

Genesis 1:1-3 has been remarkably well-preserved in the Masoretic Text (MT). However,

differences arise when comparing it to the Septuagint (LXX) and other ancient translations:

The LXX translates berēʾšît as "ἐν ἀρχῇ" ("in the beginning"), closely aligning with the MT.

Some scholars suggest that berēʾšît could imply "When God began to create," introducing a

conditional nuance absent in traditional readings. This interpretation is debated but reflected

in translations such as the New Jewish Publication Society (NJPS).

The textual integrity of the repeated ʾôr in verse 3 emphasizes the deliberate act of creation

and response.

Source:

The passage reflects the Priestly (P) source, a literary tradition known for its structured style,

repetition, and focus on God's transcendence. The P source aims to present a theologically

rich account of creation, demonstrating God's order over chaos and establishing the

framework for subsequent theological themes like Sabbath observance.

Form:

Genesis 1:1-3 fits the form of an ancient cosmogony but stands distinct in emphasizing

monotheism. Unlike surrounding Near Eastern myths (e.g., the Babylonian Enuma Elish),

this text portrays creation as ex nihilo (out of nothing) and devoid of conflict between deities.

Interpretation:

God's Sovereignty: The narrative begins with God as the initiator of creation, emphasizing

1.

His authority over all.

Order from Chaos: The transformation from tōhû wābōhû (formless and void) to structured

2.

creation highlights God's power to bring order.

Word of God: Verse 3 introduces the creative efficacy of God's spoken word, a theme

3.

echoed in John 1:1-5.

Cosmological Implications:

Ruach Elohim: The Spirit of God hovering over the waters suggests God's imminent

1.

presence and care in creation.

Light: Light, created before the sun or stars, symbolizes divine presence and order.

2.

Conclusion:

Genesis 1:1-3 provides a profound introduction to the biblical creation account. Its

theological emphasis on God's sovereignty, the power of His word, and the orderliness of

creation sets the stage for the rest of Scripture. Through careful analysis of the Hebrew text

and its literary context, these verses reveal a message of hope and divine purpose, affirming

God's ongoing relationship with creation.
